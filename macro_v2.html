GDP — Measuring the Economy

🌍 What is GDP?

GDP (Gross Domestic Product) is the total value of everything produced inside a country in one year. Think of it as the economy's "report card."

To avoid counting the same thing twice, we use value added — each firm only counts the new value it creates, not the raw materials it bought.

GDP = C + I + G + NX
C
Consumption — households buying goods & services
I
Investment — businesses buying equipment, buildings
G
Government Purchases (NOT transfer payments like pensions)
NX = X−IM
Net Exports = Exports minus Imports
⚠️ Common Exam Mistake

G does NOT include transfer payments (pensions, welfare, unemployment benefits). These are just money moving between people — no new production happens. Only spending on actual goods/services counts.

2

The Business Cycle

📈 Real vs. Potential GDP

The economy doesn't grow in a straight line — it zigzags up and down. These ups and downs are the business cycle.

Potential output (Y*) = what the economy could produce if everyone who wants a job has one at normal wages.

The Business Cycle
Actual GDP (Y)
Potential GDP (Y*)
When Y is below Y*, we have a recessionary gap. When Y is above Y*, we have an inflationary gap.
✅ Output Gaps — Simple Version
  • Y < Y* → Recessionary gap → factories idle, people unemployed → downward pressure on wages and prices
  • Y > Y* → Inflationary gap → economy overheating → upward pressure on wages → risk of inflation

Inflation & Interest Rates

CPI = (Today's basket cost ÷ Base year basket cost) × 100
Real Rate ≈ Nominal Rate − Inflation (Fisher Equation)
💡 Real vs Nominal — Why it Matters

You borrow at 8% (nominal). Inflation is 6%. Your real interest rate is only 2% — that's the actual cost to you in purchasing power terms.

The real rate is what matters for investment and borrowing decisions.

The Keynesian Cross (AE Model)

🧠 Core Idea — Demand Determines Output

In the short run, the economy produces whatever is demanded. Equilibrium happens when total planned spending equals total income/output.

Equilibrium: Y = AE
AE = C + I + G + NX
C = a + b×(Y − T)
a
Autonomous consumption (spend even with zero income)
b = MPC
Marginal Propensity to Consume: extra spending per $1 of income
Keynesian Cross — Finding Equilibrium
AE line (planned spending)
45° line (Y = AE)
Equilibrium point
Equilibrium is where the AE line crosses the 45° line. At this point, planned spending = actual output. If AE > Y, inventories fall → firms produce more → Y rises toward equilibrium.
2

The Multiplier Effect

🔄 Why a $1 Increase Creates More Than $1 of GDP

When the government spends $1 extra, that becomes someone's income. They spend part of it, which becomes someone else's income, and so on. Each round adds less (because some leaks into saving, taxes, imports).

Multiplier = 1 ÷ (1 − z)
z = MPC × (1 − tax rate) − import rate
Example
z = 0.62 → Multiplier = 1/(1−0.62) = 2.63
Meaning
Extra $1 of gov't spending → GDP rises by $2.63
The Multiplier — AE Shift
Original AE
New AE (after ↑G)
New equilibrium
A small upward shift in AE (e.g., government spends more) leads to a larger increase in equilibrium Y — that's the multiplier at work.

The AD/AS Model

📊 What This Model Shows

The AD/AS model shows the relationship between the price level (P) and real output (Y). It combines how much people want to buy (AD) with how much firms want to produce (AS).

The AD/AS Model
AS (Aggregate Supply)
AD (Aggregate Demand)
Long-run AS (Y*)
Equilibrium E₀ = where AD meets AS. The vertical line at Y* = long-run potential output. In the long run, output always returns to Y*.
⭐ Exam Critical — Stagflation

What Causes Stagflation?

A negative supply shock shifts the AS curve up and to the left. This simultaneously causes prices to rise (inflation) AND output to fall (recession). Normal demand shocks move P and Y in the same direction — supply shocks are the only way to get high inflation WITH low growth at the same time.

Stagflation — Negative Supply Shock

New AS₁ (shifted up)
AD (unchanged)
AS shifts left → new equilibrium has higher P AND lower Y. This is stagflation — the worst of both worlds.

4

Short Run → Long Run Adjustment

⏱️ The Three Time Horizons
  • Short run: Wages & prices are fixed. AD determines Y.
  • Adjustment: Wages slowly change in response to output gaps.
  • Long run: Wages fully adjust. Y always returns to Y*.
💡 Why Wages are "Sticky Downward"

Wages rise quickly when the economy is booming (workers have bargaining power). But wages fall very slowly in recessions (workers resist pay cuts). This is why recessionary gaps can persist and why government intervention may be needed.

How Banks Create Money

🏦 Fractional Reserve Banking

Banks don't keep all your deposits in a vault. They keep a small reserve (e.g., 10%) and lend out the rest. That loan becomes a deposit at another bank, which lends 90% of it out again... This creates new money!

Money Multiplier = 1 ÷ v
v = reserve ratio
Fraction of deposits kept as reserves (e.g., 10% = 0.1)
Example
v=10%, deposit $100 → $1,000 total deposits created

Monetary Transmission Mechanism

⭐ Exam Critical — Must Know This

How Rate Changes Flow Through the Economy

This is the chain reaction that connects a central bank decision to actual inflation and output. Your exam question 2 is entirely about this.

Step 1
🏛️ Central Bank raises overnight rate
e.g., to fight inflation — makes it more expensive for banks to borrow from each other
↓
Step 2
📈 All interest rates rise
Mortgage rates, business loan rates, credit card rates all follow within days
↓
Step 3
💸 Borrowing becomes expensive
Firms cancel investment projects. Households cut back on mortgages and credit purchases.
↓
Step 4 (Open Economy)
💱 Currency appreciates
Higher rates attract foreign investors → they buy domestic currency → currency gets stronger → exports fall (too expensive for foreigners) → NX falls
↓
Step 5
📉 AD shifts LEFT
Less investment + less consumption + less net exports = lower aggregate demand
↓
Step 6
👷 Y falls → unemployment rises
Firms produce less, hire fewer workers
↓
Step 7 — Goal achieved
✅ Inflation falls toward 2%
Labour market slack → workers accept lower wages → firms' costs fall → prices stabilise
3

Inflation Targeting & The Output Gap

🎯 The 2% Target

Central banks target 2% inflation. They use the output gap as an early warning signal:

  • Y > Y* (inflationary gap) → inflation likely rising → raise rates
  • Y < Y* (recessionary gap) → inflation likely falling → cut rates
💡 Long-Run Money Neutrality

In the long run, printing more money only raises prices — it doesn't increase real output. Countries with fast money supply growth always end up with high inflation. This is why central banks focus on stable money growth.

Business Cycle — Annotated
What is a Peak? ▾
The highest point of actual GDP before it starts declining. The economy is "overheating" — there may be an inflationary gap (Y > Y*). Unemployment is very low, wages are rising fast.
What is a Trough? ▾
The lowest point of actual GDP before it starts recovering. There is a recessionary gap (Y < Y*). Unemployment is high. Wages are under downward pressure. This is when fiscal/monetary stimulus is most needed.
What is NAIRU? ▾
The Natural Rate of Unemployment (or NAIRU = Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment). This is the unemployment rate when Y = Y*. It includes frictional and structural unemployment but ZERO cyclical unemployment. It's the "lowest sustainable" unemployment rate without triggering inflation.
📊 How to Read the AD/AS Graph

X-axis = real output (Y). Y-axis = price level (P). You're plotting how much the whole economy produces at each price level.

  • AS slopes upward: Firms produce more only if prices are higher (covers rising costs)
  • AD slopes downward: Higher prices reduce real wealth and purchasing power, so people demand less
  • Vertical line at Y*: Long-run AS — in the long run, output is fixed at potential
AD/AS — Full Annotated
What shifts the AD curve? ▾
AD shifts RIGHT (expansionary) when: Government spending rises (↑G), taxes cut (↓T), consumer confidence rises (↑C), exports rise (↑X), interest rates fall (↑I).

AD shifts LEFT (contractionary) when: the opposite happens, OR the central bank raises interest rates.
What shifts the AS curve? ▾
AS shifts RIGHT (expansionary) when: Input costs fall (e.g., oil price drops), wages fall, technology improves, productivity increases.

AS shifts LEFT (contractionary) when: Input costs rise (oil price spike, wage increases), natural disasters, trade restrictions. This is what causes STAGFLATION.
Why is the long-run AS vertical? ▾
In the long run, the economy always returns to Y* regardless of the price level. Even if prices double, the economy's real productive capacity (workers, factories, technology) hasn't changed. So in the long run, P can vary but Y stays at Y*.
⚡ Stagflation — Step by Step

Stagflation is caused by a negative supply shock. Here's exactly what happens on the graph:

  1. Start at equilibrium E₀ (where AD meets AS₀)
  2. Negative supply shock hits (e.g., oil prices surge)
  3. AS shifts LEFT to AS₁ (firms need higher prices to produce same output)
  4. New equilibrium E₁: P rises AND Y falls
  5. Result: Inflation + recession = STAGFLATION
Negative Supply Shock → Stagflation
Why is stagflation so hard to fix? ▾
Normal policy tools only shift AD. To fix inflation, you shift AD left (raise rates) — but that makes the recession worse. To fix the recession, you shift AD right (cut rates) — but that makes inflation worse. You can't fix both with AD policy alone. This is Roubini's "stagflation trap."
Real world examples of stagflation ▾
1970s: OPEC oil embargo → oil prices quadrupled → AS shock in all oil-importing countries → stagflation across US, Europe.

2021–2022: COVID supply chain disruptions + Russia-Ukraine war → energy prices soared → stagflation risk returned → this is exactly the context of the Roubini article in your exam.
🔄 The Multiplier — Visual Explanation

The government spends an extra $100. Where does it go?

  1. Gov spends $100 → becomes $100 of income for workers
  2. Workers (MPC=0.8) spend $80 of it → $80 new income for others
  3. Those people spend $64 → $64 new income...
  4. ...and so on until the rounds become tiny
  5. Total GDP increase = $100 + $80 + $64 + ... = $500 (with multiplier=5)
Multiplier — AE Model
AE₀ (original)
AE₁ (after ↑G)
Notice how the horizontal shift in Y (ΔY) is much larger than the upward shift in AE (ΔA). That's the multiplier.
What reduces the multiplier? ▾
Three "leakages" reduce the multiplier:

1. Saving: Every dollar not spent leaks out (MPS = 1-MPC)
2. Taxes: Government takes a share of income before spending
3. Imports: Spending on foreign goods doesn't circulate in the domestic economy

Higher taxes or higher import dependency = smaller multiplier = less effective fiscal policy.
Paradox of Thrift ▾
If everyone individually decides to save more at the same time, total spending falls → AE shifts down → equilibrium GDP falls → everyone ends up worse off (and may save less than intended because income fell!)

What's rational for one person is harmful for the whole economy in the short run. This is why in recessions, governments increase spending even as households cut back.

THE ROUBINI QUOTE (your exam context)

"Central banks are in both a stagflation trap and a debt trap. They are damned if they do and damned if they don't. If they increase interest rates enough to bring inflation down to 2%, they will cause a severe economic hard landing. And if they don't – attempting instead to protect growth and jobs – they will be left increasingly far behind the curve, leading to a de-anchoring of inflation expectations and a wage-price spiral."

QUESTION 1
What is stagflation? What type of shock causes it?
5 pts
▾

Definition: Stagflation = stagnation + inflation. The simultaneous occurrence of high/rising inflation AND low/negative economic growth AND high unemployment.

Why it's unusual: Normal demand shocks move P and Y in the same direction. Stagflation moves them in opposite directions — prices go up while output goes down.

Cause — Negative Supply Shock: Something that increases production costs economy-wide, shifting the AS curve up and to the left.

  • Oil price spike (OPEC 1973, Russia-Ukraine 2022)
  • Global supply chain disruption (COVID-19)
  • Trade tariffs raising input costs

On the AD/AS graph: AS shifts left → new equilibrium has BOTH higher P (inflation) and lower Y (recession). This is structurally impossible to fix with demand-side policy alone.

QUESTION 2
CB response to reduce inflation to 2% — explain the transmission mechanism
5 pts
▾

Policy: Contractionary monetary policy — raise the overnight interest rate target.

Transmission mechanism (step by step):

  1. CB raises overnight rate → commercial banks' borrowing costs rise immediately
  2. All market rates follow: mortgages, business loans, consumer credit all rise within days
  3. Investment falls (↓I): Firms postpone or cancel capital projects (cost of borrowing too high)
  4. Consumption falls (↓C): Households cut back on mortgage-financed purchases, credit spending
  5. Open-economy channel: Higher domestic rates attract foreign capital → currency appreciates → exports become expensive → NX falls
  6. AD shifts left: Combined fall in I, C, and NX reduces aggregate demand
  7. Y falls below Y*: Recessionary gap opens → cyclical unemployment rises
  8. Labour market slackens: Workers accept lower wages → firms' costs fall → AS shifts back down → inflation falls toward 2%
QUESTION 3
What does "behind the curve" mean?
5 pts
▾

Core meaning: The central bank is "behind the curve" when it has been too slow to raise interest rates, allowing inflation to become entrenched before acting.

The mechanism — de-anchoring of expectations:

  • If the CB delays hiking rates, inflation keeps rising
  • People and firms start to expect high inflation to continue → they build it into their behaviour
  • Workers demand higher nominal wages to protect their real wages
  • Firms raise prices to cover higher wage costs
  • Workers then demand even higher wages… → wage-price spiral

Why "curve"? The CB's policy rate should be tracking the inflation path. If it doesn't keep up, it falls further and further "behind" what's needed — like running after a moving bus.

Consequence: Once expectations are de-anchored, the CB needs to raise rates even more aggressively than if it had acted earlier — causing more economic pain than would have been necessary.

QUESTION 4
Why does hiking rates cause economic AND financial crash?
5 pts
▾

Economic crash (via transmission mechanism from Q2): Sharply higher interest rates → severe contraction in I, C, NX → AD collapses → deep recession → mass unemployment. The more delayed the hikes, the more aggressive they must be, the deeper the recession.

Financial crash (Roubini's additional point — the debt trap):

  • Years of very low interest rates led to massive debt accumulation (private and public)
  • When rates rise sharply, debt servicing costs explode for highly leveraged borrowers
  • Private sector: Over-indebted firms → cash flow crunch → corporate defaults → bank losses
  • Public sector: High-debt governments → interest payments surge → fiscal crisis
  • Asset prices crash: Bonds fall (inversely related to rates), property markets collapse
  • Financial turmoil amplifies recession: Credit crunch → less lending → less investment → deeper recession → more defaults → more financial instability. A vicious cycle.

Key insight: This is different from a normal monetary tightening — the combination of stagflation + extreme debt levels makes the CB's dilemma uniquely painful.

QUESTION 5
Why does NOT hiking lead to permanently higher inflation?
5 pts
▾

Three mechanisms work together:

1. De-anchoring of inflation expectations:

  • If the CB doesn't act, people stop believing the 2% target will hold
  • They build higher expected inflation into wages, contracts, and pricing
  • Expected inflation → actual inflation (self-fulfilling prophecy)

2. Wage-price spiral (see Q3):

  • Higher prices → workers demand higher wages → firms raise prices → repeat
  • Once started, very hard to stop without causing a severe recession

3. Loss of central bank credibility:

  • CB credibility is the anchor for inflation expectations
  • If CB is seen as too weak to fight inflation, long-run inflation expectations shift permanently higher
  • Restoring credibility later requires an even more painful disinflation (1980s Volcker shock: rates hit 20%, causing deep recession)

Historical lesson: The 1970s showed that delaying action embeds inflation deeply. The cure (massive rate hikes) became far more painful than early action would have been.
