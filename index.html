GDP — Measuring the Economy

🌍 What is GDP?

GDP = total value of all goods & services produced inside a country in one year. We count only value added at each stage to avoid double-counting.

GDP = C + I + G + NX
C
Consumption — household spending
I
Investment — business equipment, buildings
G
Gov't purchases (NOT pensions/welfare)
NX = X−IM
Exports minus Imports
⚠️ Common Exam Mistake

G does NOT include transfer payments (pensions, unemployment benefits). No new production = not in GDP.

2

Business Cycle & Output Gaps

Potential output (Y*) = what the economy produces at full employment. Actual GDP fluctuates around Y* — that's the business cycle.

  • Y < Y* → Recessionary gap → high unemployment → downward wage pressure
  • Y > Y* → Inflationary gap → overheating → inflation risk

NAIRU = unemployment when Y=Y* (frictional + structural only, zero cyclical). Lowest sustainable rate without causing inflation.

Inflation & Interest Rates

Real Rate ≈ Nominal Rate − Inflation
💡 Why it matters

Borrow at 8%, inflation 6% → real cost = 2%. Investment decisions depend on the real rate. CB targets inflation to keep real rates stable and predictable.

1

The AE Model (Keynesian Cross)

🧠 Core Idea

In the short run, output is demand-determined. The economy produces whatever people plan to spend. Equilibrium = planned spending equals actual output (Y = AE).

AE = C + I + G + NX
C = a + MPC × (Y − T)
a
Autonomous consumption (spend even with zero income)
MPC
Fraction of each extra $1 income that gets spent
2

The Multiplier

🔄 Why $1 creates more than $1 of GDP

Gov spends $100 → someone's income → they spend $80 → that's income for others → they spend $64... Each round creates less until the ripple fades. Total GDP rise = much more than $100.

Multiplier = 1 ÷ (1 − z)
z = 0.62
→ Multiplier = 1/0.38 = 2.63
Meaning
$1 gov spending → $2.63 extra GDP
3

The AD/AS Model

AS slopes up: firms need higher prices to justify producing more.
AD slopes down: higher prices reduce real wealth, less demanded.
Y* vertical: long-run output — economy always returns here.

⭐ Exam Critical — Stagflation

What Causes Stagflation?

A negative supply shock shifts AS left/upward — simultaneously raising prices AND cutting output. Normal demand-side policy cannot fix both at once. This is the central bank's impossible dilemma.

How Banks Create Money

🏦 Fractional Reserve Banking

Banks keep a reserve ratio (v) and lend the rest. That loan becomes a deposit, which is lent again... money multiplies!

Money Multiplier = 1 ÷ v
v = 10%
$100 → $1,000 total deposits created
v = 20%

Inflation Targeting

🎯 The 2% Target
  • Y > Y* → CB raises rates (fight inflation)
  • Y < Y* → CB cuts rates (boost growth)
  • Output gap = early warning signal for future inflation
Long-Run Money Neutrality

In the long run, printing more money only raises prices — real output always returns to Y*. CBs focus on inflation targets, not money supply growth.

X = Time. Y = Real GDP. The purple curve is actual output. The dashed red line is potential output (Y*). When actual is above Y* → inflationary gap. When below → recessionary gap.

Business Cycle
What is a Peak? ▾
Highest point before GDP declines. Economy is overheating — inflationary gap (Y>Y*). Unemployment very low, wages rising fast. CB may need to raise rates.
What is a Trough? ▾
Lowest point before recovery. Recessionary gap (Y<Y*). High unemployment, weak wages. Gov may use fiscal stimulus or CB may cut rates.
What is NAIRU? ▾
Natural Rate of Unemployment when Y=Y*. Includes frictional + structural unemployment only — zero cyclical. The healthiest achievable state. Some unemployment always exists naturally.
How to read the AD/AS graph

X = Real Output (Y). Y = Price Level (P).

● AS slopes up: higher prices needed to cover rising costs.
● AD slopes down: higher prices → less real wealth → less demand.
● Y* is vertical: long-run potential — output always returns here.

AD / AS — Normal Equilibrium
What shifts AD? ▾
AD right (expansionary): ↑G, ↓T, ↑consumer confidence, ↓interest rates, ↑exports.

AD left (contractionary): CB raises interest rates, ↑T, ↓G, ↓confidence, ↓exports.
What shifts AS? ▾
AS right (good): ↓oil prices, ↓wages, better technology, ↑productivity.

AS left (bad — causes stagflation): ↑oil prices, supply chain disruption, ↑wages, trade tariffs.
Why is long-run AS vertical? ▾
In the long run, the economy's real productive capacity (workers, technology, capital) doesn't change just because prices changed. Output gravitates back to Y* through wage adjustment regardless of the price level.
⚡ Stagflation — what happens on the graph
  1. Start at equilibrium E₀ (AD meets AS₀)
  2. Negative supply shock hits (oil spike, supply chain crisis)
  3. Firms' costs rise → AS shifts left to AS₁
  4. New equilibrium E₁: P rises AND Y falls
  5. Result = Stagflation
Negative Supply Shock → Stagflation
AS₀ shifts left to AS₁ → new equilibrium E₁ has higher prices AND lower output simultaneously.
Why can't normal policy fix stagflation? ▾
Demand-side tools only shift AD. To fight inflation → shift AD left (raise rates) → makes recession worse. To fight recession → shift AD right (cut rates) → makes inflation worse. You cannot fix both simultaneously. This is Roubini's "stagflation trap."
Real-world examples ▾
1970s: OPEC oil embargo → oil prices quadrupled → AS shock across oil-importing countries → stagflation in US and Europe.

2021–22: COVID supply chains + Russia-Ukraine war → energy prices soared. Exact context of your Roubini exam article.
How to read this graph

X = Y (income). Y = AE (planned spending).

The 45° line = where Y=AE (equilibrium). The AE line has slope = MPC < 1. Where they intersect = equilibrium income. When AE shifts up by ΔA, equilibrium rises by ΔY — which is much bigger. That gap is the multiplier.

The Multiplier — Keynesian Cross
Small upward shift ΔA in AE → much larger rise ΔY in equilibrium income. The ratio ΔY/ΔA = the multiplier.
What reduces the multiplier? ▾
Three "leakages" each round:
  • Saving: Income not spent (MPS = 1−MPC)
  • Taxes: Gov takes a share before re-spending
  • Imports: Spending on foreign goods doesn't circulate domestically
Higher taxes or import-dependency = smaller multiplier = less effective fiscal policy.
Paradox of Thrift ▾
If everyone saves more simultaneously → AE shifts down → GDP falls → incomes fall. Individual prudence = collective harm in the short run. That's why governments spend more during recessions even as households cut back.

THE ROUBINI QUOTE (your exam context)

"Central banks are in both a stagflation trap and a debt trap. They are damned if they do and damned if they don't. If they increase interest rates enough to bring inflation down to 2%, they will cause a severe economic hard landing. And if they don't – attempting instead to protect growth and jobs – they will be left increasingly far behind the curve, leading to a de-anchoring of inflation expectations and a wage-price spiral."

Q1
What is stagflation? What shock causes it?
5 pts
▾

Definition: Stagflation = stagnation + inflation. Simultaneous (1) high/rising inflation, (2) low/negative growth, (3) high unemployment.

Why unusual: Normal demand shocks move P and Y in the same direction. Stagflation moves them in opposite directions — prices up, output down.

Cause — Negative Supply Shock: Something that raises firms' costs economy-wide, shifting AS upward/leftward. Examples: oil price spike (OPEC 1973, Russia-Ukraine 2022), supply chain disruption (COVID), trade tariffs on inputs.

On the AD/AS graph: AS shifts left → new equilibrium has BOTH higher P (inflation) AND lower Y (recession). Demand-side policy alone cannot fix both.

Q2
CB response to reduce inflation to 2% — explain the transmission mechanism
5 pts
▾

Policy: Contractionary monetary policy — raise the overnight interest rate target.

Transmission mechanism step by step:

  1. CB raises overnight rate → commercial bank borrowing costs rise immediately
  2. All market rates follow — mortgages, business loans, credit cards rise within days
  3. ↓ Investment (I): Firms postpone/cancel capital projects
  4. ↓ Consumption (C): Households cut mortgage-financed and credit purchases
  5. Open-economy channel: Higher rates attract foreign capital → currency appreciates → exports expensive → NX falls
  6. AD shifts left: Combined fall in I + C + NX
  7. Y falls below Y*: Recessionary gap opens, unemployment rises
  8. Labour market slackens: Lower wage demands → lower costs → inflation falls toward 2%
Q3
What does "behind the curve" mean?
5 pts
▾

Core meaning: The CB was too slow to raise rates, allowing inflation to become entrenched. Its policy rate is below where it needs to be to control inflation.

The de-anchoring mechanism:

  • CB delays hiking → inflation keeps rising
  • People stop believing the 2% target will hold
  • Workers demand higher wages to protect real wages
  • Firms raise prices to cover higher labour costs
  • Workers demand even more → wage-price spiral
  • Now the CB needs even bigger hikes than if it had acted early — causing more pain

Analogy: Like chasing a moving bus — the longer you wait to run, the faster you need to sprint, and the harder the fall.

Q4
Why do rate hikes cause economic AND financial crash?
5 pts
▾

Economic crash (via Q2 transmission mechanism): Sharp rate hikes → deep contraction in I, C, NX → AD collapses → severe recession → mass unemployment. The more delayed the hike, the more aggressive it must be, the deeper the recession.

Financial crash — the debt trap:

  • Years of ultra-low rates → massive debt accumulation (private and public)
  • Sharp rate rises → debt servicing costs explode for leveraged borrowers
  • Private sector: Over-indebted firms → cash flow crunch → defaults → bank losses
  • Public sector: High-debt governments → interest payments surge → fiscal crisis
  • Asset prices crash: Bonds fall (inverse of rates), property collapses
  • Vicious cycle: Financial turmoil → credit crunch → deeper recession → more defaults → more turmoil
Q5
Why does NOT hiking lead to permanently higher inflation?
5 pts
▾

Three mechanisms work together:

1. De-anchoring of expectations: If CB doesn't act, people stop believing the 2% target. They build higher expected inflation into wages and contracts. Expected inflation → actual inflation (self-fulfilling prophecy).

2. Wage-price spiral: Rising prices → workers demand higher wages → firms raise prices → workers demand more → repeat. Very hard to stop once started without causing recession.

3. Loss of CB credibility: Credibility is the anchor for expectations. Once lost, restoring it requires a much more painful disinflation — like Volcker's 20% rates in 1980 which caused a deep recession.

Historical lesson: The 1970s showed that delay embeds inflation permanently. The cure becomes far more painful than early action would have been.
