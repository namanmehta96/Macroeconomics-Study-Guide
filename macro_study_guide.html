Day 1

Key Macroeconomic Variables & National Accounting

1. GDP — The Big Number

Core Definition

Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

GDP = the total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders in a given period. It equals the sum of value added at every stage of production — this avoids double counting.

Value Added = Revenue − Cost of Intermediate Inputs GDP = Sum of all Value Added in the Economy
3 Ways to Measure GDP — All Give the Same Answer

The Three Approaches

  1. Expenditure approach: GDP = C + I + G + NX
    Consumption + Investment + Gov't Purchases + Net Exports
  2. Income approach: GDP = Net Domestic Income + Indirect Taxes + Depreciation
  3. Value Added approach: Sum up each firm's contribution

Because of the circular flow: Production = Income = Expenditure

GDP Components Explained

ComponentSymbolIncludesExcludes
ConsumptionCHousehold spending on goods & services—
InvestmentIBusiness equipment, inventories, residential constructionFinancial investments (stocks/bonds)
Gov't PurchasesGGov't goods & services, militaryTransfer payments (pensions, welfare)

Important Distinction

GDP vs. GNP

GDP = output produced within a country's borders (location-based)

GNP = output produced by a country's residents, regardless of location

Example: Toyota factory in Canada → counted in Canada's GDP but NOT in Canada's GNP (profits go to Japanese owners)

2. Real vs. Nominal

Crucial Distinction

Nominal GDP: Measured in current prices. Goes up even if only prices rose (no real growth).

Real GDP: Measured in constant base-year prices. Only rises if actual output increases. This is what matters for living standards.

Real GDP Growth = Nominal GDP Growth − Inflation

3. The Business Cycle

The Business Cycle: Actual GDP fluctuates around Potential GDP (Y*). Gaps matter for policy.
Output Gaps
  • Recessionary gap: Y < Y* → unemployment above NAIRU, downward pressure on wages/prices
  • Inflationary gap: Y > Y* → tight labour markets, upward pressure on wages/prices → risk of inflation

4. Unemployment

Unemployment Rate = (# Unemployed / # in Labour Force) × 100 Labour Force = Employed + Unemployed
TypeCauseExample
FrictionalNormal job search timeGraduate looking for first job
StructuralSkill mismatch / industry shiftCoal miner in a green economy
CyclicalRecession — insufficient demandFactory worker laid off in downturn
Key Term

NAIRU = Natural Rate of Unemployment

The unemployment rate when Y = Y*. Includes frictional + structural, but NOT cyclical. Zero cyclical unemployment is the goal, not zero unemployment.

5. Inflation & Interest Rates

CPI Formula
CPI = (ΣP_t × Q_0) / (ΣP_0 × Q_0) × 100 Where Q_0 = base year quantities, P_t = current prices, P_0 = base year prices

Inflation rate = % change in CPI from one period to the next.

Real vs Nominal Interest Rate
Fisher Equation: Real Interest Rate ≈ Nominal Interest Rate − Inflation Rate r ≈ i − π

The real rate is what matters for borrowing decisions — it reflects actual purchasing power cost of debt.

6. Exchange Rates & Balance of Payments

Exchange rate: # of domestic currency units to buy 1 unit of foreign currency.

Depreciation = domestic currency worth less (exchange rate rises). Improves exports, hurts imports.

Appreciation = domestic currency worth more. Hurts exports, helps imports.

Balance of Payments: CA + KA = 0 (Current Account + Capital Account = 0 — always!)

A current account deficit (imports > exports) means you're borrowing from the world → capital account surplus.

Day 2

Short-Run Macro Theory, AD/AS Model & Fiscal Policy

1. The AE Model (Keynesian Cross)

Core Idea

In the short run, output is demand-determined. Firms produce whatever is demanded. Equilibrium occurs when what people want to spend equals what is actually produced.

Equilibrium: Y = AE(Y) AE = C + I + G + (X − IM)
Consumption Function
C = a + b × YD where: a = autonomous consumption (spending even at zero income) b = MPC (Marginal Propensity to Consume) = ΔC/ΔYD YD = Disposable Income = Y − T 0 < b < 1

MPC tells us: for every extra $1 of income, how much goes to consumption. The rest goes to saving: MPS = 1 − MPC

Full AE Function (with government & trade)
AE = [a + I + G + X] + [b(1−t) − m] × Y ↑ Autonomous ↑ Induced (slope = z) where: t = net tax rate (T = tY) m = marginal propensity to import (IM = mY) z = b(1−t) − m ← slope of AE function

2. The Multiplier

Keynesian Cross: A rise in autonomous spending (ΔA) shifts AE up. Equilibrium income rises by more than ΔA — the multiplier effect.
The Simple Multiplier
Simple Multiplier = 1 / (1 − z) where z = marginal propensity to spend = b(1−t) − m Example: if z = 0.62 → Multiplier = 1/(1−0.62) = 2.63 An extra $1 of government spending → GDP rises by $2.63

The multiplier works because spending becomes someone else's income, which becomes more spending. Each "round" adds less (because some leaks into saving, taxes, imports).

3. The AD/AS Model

AD/AS Model: Equilibrium at E₀ where AD meets AS. Y* is potential output (long-run AS is vertical at Y*).
Shock TypeCauseEffect on PEffect on Y
Expansionary AD shock↑G, ↑I, ↑C, ↑X↑↑
Contractionary AD shock↓G, ↓I, ↓C, ↓X↓↓
Expansionary AS shock↓input costs, better tech↓↑
Contractionary AS shock↑oil prices, ↑wages↑↓
⭐ KEY EXAM CONCEPT — STAGFLATION

What is Stagflation?

Stagflation = Stagnation + Inflation. A situation where the economy simultaneously experiences:

  • High / rising inflation (↑P)
  • Low growth or recession (↓Y)
  • High unemployment

Cause: A negative supply shock — something that raises firms' costs (e.g., oil price spike, supply chain disruption). This shifts the AS curve upward/leftward, simultaneously pushing prices up AND output down.

Negative Supply Shock → AS shifts LEFT/UP → P rises AND Y falls → STAGFLATION

4. Short-Run to Long-Run Adjustment

The Adjustment Process
SituationWhat HappensLong-Run Result
Y > Y* (Inflationary gap)Labour tight → wages rise → AS shifts upY returns to Y*, P higher
Y < Y* (Recessionary gap)Labour slack → wages fall (slowly) → AS shifts downY returns to Y*, P lower

Key insight: Wages are sticky downward — they rise fast in booms but fall slowly in recessions. This is why recessionary gaps persist and fiscal/monetary policy may be needed.

5. Fiscal Policy

Definition

Fiscal Policy = Using G and T to influence Y

  • Expansionary: ↑G or ↓T → shift AD right → close recessionary gap
  • Contractionary: ↓G or ↑T → shift AD left → close inflationary gap

Paradox of Thrift: If everyone saves more simultaneously, aggregate demand falls → GDP falls → everyone ends up worse off. Good for individuals, bad for the economy in the short run.

Limitations of Fiscal Policy
  • Decision lag: Time to recognize the problem and decide what to do
  • Execution lag: Time to implement the policy
  • Effect lag: Time for the policy to actually affect the economy
  • By the time it works, the economy may have already recovered → could cause inflationary gap

Day 3

Money, Banking & Monetary Policy

1. What is Money?

Money serves 3 functions:

  1. Medium of exchange: Accepted for goods/services → eliminates barter
  2. Unit of account: Common measure of value
  3. Store of value: Preserves purchasing power over time (works when inflation is low)

Fiat money: No intrinsic value — backed by government decree. Modern currencies are fiat money.

2. Money Creation by Banks

Fractional Reserve Banking

Banks hold only a fraction of deposits as reserves and lend the rest. This creates new money.

Money Multiplier = 1/v (no cash drain) = 1/(c+v) (with cash drain) where: v = reserve ratio (fraction of deposits kept as reserves) c = currency-deposit ratio Example: v = 20% → $100 deposit → $500 total deposits created $100 × (1/0.20) = $500

3. The Monetary Transmission Mechanism

⭐ KEY EXAM CONCEPT — Transmission Mechanism

How Central Bank Rate Changes Affect the Economy

Contractionary Monetary Policy Transmission: Higher rates → lower investment & consumption → lower AD → lower output → lower inflation
Open Economy Addition

When interest rates rise, foreign investors bring money in (seeking higher returns) → domestic currency appreciates → exports become more expensive for foreigners → net exports fall → additional downward pressure on AD.

↑i → foreign capital inflow → currency appreciates → ↓NX → ↓AD

4. Inflation Targeting

Modern Central Bank Framework

Most central banks (Fed, ECB, Bank of Canada) target 2% inflation.

  • If output gap is positive (Y > Y*): inflation tends to rise → CB raises rates (contractionary)
  • If output gap is negative (Y < Y*): inflation tends to fall → CB cuts rates (expansionary)

The CB monitors the output gap as an indicator of future inflation pressure.

5. Long-Run Money Neutrality

In the long run, changes in money supply do not affect real variables (real GDP, real wages, unemployment). They only affect the price level.

Evidence: Countries with faster money growth → higher inflation (but not necessarily higher real growth).

Long Run: ↑Money Supply → ↑P (proportionally), no change in Y Short Run: ↑Money Supply → ↑AD → ↑Y and ↑P (both)

🎯 Exam Prep

Your actual exam questions with model answers

Context — The Roubini Quote (2022)

"Central banks are in both a stagflation trap and a debt trap. They are damned if they do and damned if they don't. If they increase interest rates enough to bring inflation down to 2%, they will cause a severe economic hard landing. And if they don't – attempting instead to protect growth and jobs – they will be left increasingly far behind the curve, leading to a de-anchoring of inflation expectations and a wage-price spiral."

Q1 (5pts): What is stagflation? What shock causes it?

📝 Model Answer Framework

Definition: Stagflation is the simultaneous occurrence of high (or rising) inflation and economic stagnation (low or negative growth + high unemployment). The term combines "stagnation" and "inflation."

Shock: A negative supply shock — something that increases production costs for firms economy-wide. This shifts the AS curve upward and to the left.

Examples of negative supply shocks: An oil price spike (1970s OPEC crisis), a global supply chain disruption (COVID-19), rising input prices due to trade tariffs.

AD/AS explanation: When AS shifts left, two things happen simultaneously: the price level rises (inflation) AND equilibrium output falls (recession). This is unique — normal demand shocks move P and Y in the same direction, but supply shocks move them in opposite directions.

Why Roubini used this term (2022): Post-COVID supply disruptions + energy price spike from Russia-Ukraine war created exactly these conditions: rising prices + slowing growth.

Q2 (5pts): CB response to reduce inflation to 2% — Transmission mechanism

📝 Model Answer Framework

Policy: Contractionary monetary policy — the central bank raises its target for the overnight interest rate.

Transmission mechanism (step by step):

  1. CB raises overnight rate → other short-term rates follow immediately → longer-term rates adjust too (mortgage rates, corporate borrowing rates)
  2. Higher borrowing cost → ↓ Investment (I) and ↓ Consumption (C): Firms cancel or postpone projects; households cut back on credit-financed spending
  3. → AE shifts down → AD shifts left: Equilibrium GDP falls
  4. Open-economy channel: Higher domestic rates attract foreign capital → CAD appreciates → exports become expensive → NX falls → additional leftward shift in AD
  5. Y falls below Y* → recessionary gap opens → labour market loosens → wage demands fall → firms' costs fall → AS shifts downward → inflation falls
  6. Result: Lower inflation, but at the cost of higher unemployment and lower output

Q3 (5pts): What does "behind the curve" mean?

📝 Model Answer Framework

Core meaning: "Behind the curve" means the central bank has been too slow to raise interest rates, allowing inflation to become entrenched before acting. The CB's policy rate is below where it needs to be to control inflation.

Full explanation:

  • If the CB hesitates and doesn't raise rates (to avoid hurting growth), inflation continues rising
  • As inflation persists, inflation expectations become de-anchored: households and firms begin expecting high inflation to continue
  • Workers demand higher wages to compensate → firms face higher costs → they raise prices → a wage-price spiral begins
  • Once expectations become unanchored, controlling inflation requires even larger rate increases than would have been needed if the CB had acted earlier

Roubini's point: By protecting growth and jobs in the short run (not hiking), the CB falls further and further "behind" — the inflation problem gets worse, not better. Eventually it will need to act even more aggressively.

Q4 (5pts): Why does CB rate hikes cause economic AND financial crash?

📝 Model Answer Framework

Economic crash mechanism (from Q2):

  • Sharply higher interest rates → severe contraction in investment and consumption → AD collapses → deep recession → mass unemployment
  • This is the "hard landing" — the more delayed the hike, the more aggressive it must be, and the more damaging

Financial crash mechanism (Roubini's addition):

  • After years of low rates, governments, households, and firms accumulated very high levels of debt
  • When rates rise sharply, debt servicing costs rise dramatically — highly leveraged borrowers face distress
  • Private sector: Firms with floating-rate debt → cash flow crunch → defaults → bank losses
  • Public sector: Governments with high debt → interest payments explode → fiscal pressure → possible sovereign debt crises
  • Financial turmoil intensifies the recession (vicious cycle): Financial instability → credit crunch → less lending → less investment → deeper recession → more defaults → more financial instability

Bottom line: The CB faces a dilemma — hiking enough to kill inflation causes a recession and a financial crisis because of the debt overhang built up during low-rate years.

Q5 (5pts): Why does NOT hiking lead to permanently higher inflation?

📝 Model Answer Framework

Three mechanisms:

1. De-anchoring of inflation expectations:

  • If the CB doesn't act credibly, people stop believing the 2% target will be maintained
  • Workers and firms build higher inflation into their contracts and pricing decisions
  • This creates self-fulfilling inflation: expected inflation → actual inflation

2. Wage-price spiral:

  • Workers see prices rising → demand higher wages → firms' costs rise → firms raise prices → further inflation → workers demand even higher wages… spiral continues

3. Loss of CB credibility:

  • Central bank credibility is crucial for keeping inflation expectations anchored
  • If the CB is seen as unwilling to fight inflation, agents update their beliefs about the long-run inflation rate upward
  • Once credibility is lost, regaining it requires a very painful disinflation (like Volcker shock in the early 1980s, which caused deep recession)

Historical lesson: The 1970s showed that delaying action against inflation embedded it deeply. The cure (Volcker's massive rate hikes) was more painful than the disease would have been if treated earlier.
