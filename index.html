GDP — What Is the Economy?

Definition
GDP (Gross Domestic Product)
The total value of everything produced in a country in one year. Think of it as the economy's "score" — a single number that tells you how big and active the economy is. Higher GDP = more stuff produced = more jobs = more income.
🧱 Why does GDP = Income?

Every time something is produced, that production creates income for someone. A bakery makes $100 worth of bread → $100 becomes wages for the baker, profit for the owner, payment to the flour supplier. So: National Product = National Income. Always. By definition.

GDP = C + I + G + NX
C — Consumption
Households buying things: food, rent, phones, cars. ~60% of GDP. The biggest piece.
I — Investment
Businesses buying machines, buildings, equipment to produce more. NOT stocks or bonds.
G — Gov't Spending
Government buying goods/services: roads, hospitals, defence. NOT pensions or welfare.
NX = Exports − Imports
Foreigners buying our stuff (exports) minus us buying their stuff (imports).
⚠️ Exam Trap #1 — Transfer Payments are NOT in GDP. Pensions, welfare, unemployment benefits, student grants — these are just the government moving money from taxpayers to someone else. No new good or service is created. So they're NOT in G and NOT in GDP. Only actual purchases count.
💡 Nominal vs Real GDP — The Key Difference

Nominal GDP = output measured at today's prices. Goes up even if you just had inflation — no extra stuff produced.

Real GDP = output measured at constant (base year) prices. Strips out inflation. This is the one that actually matters.

Example: Economy produces 100 apples. Last year they cost $1 each (GDP=$100). This year they cost $2 each. Nominal GDP = $200. Real GDP = still $100. Nothing extra was produced.

The Business Cycle — Why the Economy Goes Up and Down

📈 The core idea

The economy never grows in a straight line. It expands, overheats, contracts, and recovers in waves. This is normal. The key concept is comparing actual output (Y) to potential output (Y*).

Definition
Y = Actual GDP (what we actually produce)
The real GDP right now. Changes constantly with the business cycle — goes up in booms, falls in recessions.
Definition
Y* = Potential Output (what we COULD produce)
What the economy would produce if every worker who wants a job has one, and every factory runs at normal capacity. It grows slowly over time as technology improves and population grows. Y* is the economy's "speed limit."
The Two Output Gaps
  • Recessionary Gap: Y < Y* — Economy below potential. Unemployment HIGH, wages weak, inflation LOW. Policy response: CB cuts rates OR government increases spending to boost demand.
  • Inflationary Gap: Y > Y* — Economy above potential. Overheating. Unemployment very LOW, wages rising fast, inflation building. Policy response: CB raises rates to cool things down.
📊 The Business Cycle
The economy always oscillates around Y*. At the peak (Y>Y*) inflation is the risk. At the trough (Y<Y*) unemployment is the problem. Policy tries to smooth these swings.

Unemployment

Unemployment Rate = (Unemployed ÷ Labour Force) × 100

Labour Force = employed + unemployed people actively looking for work. Does NOT include: retired people, students, or "discouraged workers" (people who gave up looking).

Example: Country has 1000 workers. 940 have jobs. 60 are looking for work. Labour Force = 1000. Unemployment Rate = 60/1000 × 100 = 6%.

TypeWhat it isExampleFix?

Definition
NAIRU — Natural Rate of Unemployment
The unemployment rate when Y = Y*. Only frictional + structural unemployment exist. Zero cyclical unemployment. Typically ~4–6% in developed economies. This is the lowest sustainable rate — going below it means the economy is overheating and inflation will accelerate.
4

Inflation

Definition
Inflation
A sustained rise in the general price level. Measured by the CPI (Consumer Price Index) — the cost of a typical basket of goods and services compared to a base year.
CPI = (Cost of basket today ÷ Cost in base year) × 100
Real Interest Rate ≈ Nominal Rate − Inflation
💡 Why real rate matters more than nominal

You borrow at 8% nominal. Inflation is 6%. Real cost = only 2%. The bank gets back less purchasing power than it lent out — debt erodes in real terms. All investment decisions are made on the real rate. CBs raise nominal rates precisely to raise the real cost of borrowing.

Demand-Pull vs Cost-Push Inflation
  • Demand-pull: Too much spending (AD shifts right). Economy overheating, Y > Y*. Classic "too much money chasing too few goods."
  • Cost-push: Production costs rise (oil price spike, wages surge), forcing firms to raise prices even as output falls. This is stagflation territory — AS shifts left.

The AE Model — How GDP Is Determined

What is AE?
AE = Aggregate Expenditure (Total Planned Spending)
The total amount everyone in the economy plans to spend. AE = C + I + G + NX. This is the demand side. The key insight: in the short run, the economy produces whatever is demanded. So AE determines Y.
🧠 The Big Idea — Demand Drives Output (Short Run)

Imagine a bakery. If more customers walk in (more demand), the baker bakes more bread (more output). If fewer customers come, less is baked. The whole economy works the same way in the short run: more spending → more production → higher GDP.

This is the Keynesian view — named after John Maynard Keynes who argued governments should spend during recessions to boost demand and pull the economy out.

Equilibrium: Y = AE
AE = C + I + G + NX
C = a + MPC × (1−t) × Y
MPC
Marginal Propensity to Consume. How much of each extra $1 of income you spend. e.g. MPC=0.8 means you spend 80 cents.
t
Tax rate. Reduces disposable income. If t=0.2, you keep 80% of income to spend.
a
Autonomous consumption. What you spend even if income = 0 (dipping into savings).
z = MPC(1−t) − m
The slope of the AE line. m = marginal propensity to import.
📊 Keynesian Cross — Equilibrium
Small ΔA (e.g. gov spends $100 more) → AE line shifts UP → new equilibrium Y₁ is much further right. That's the multiplier — each $1 of spending creates more than $1 of GDP.

The Multiplier — Why $1 of Gov Spending Creates More Than $1

🔄 The Chain Reaction

Government spends $100 building a road. The construction worker earns $100. She spends $80 at a restaurant (MPC=0.8). The waiter earns $80, spends $64... and so on. The initial $100 ripples through the economy and creates much more than $100 of total GDP.

Multiplier = 1 ÷ (1 − z)
z = MPC × (1 − tax rate) − import rate
Example calculation

MPC = 0.8, tax rate = 0.1, import rate = 0.1
z = 0.8 × (1 − 0.1) − 0.1 = 0.8 × 0.9 − 0.1 = 0.72 − 0.1 = 0.62
Multiplier = 1 ÷ (1 − 0.62) = 1 ÷ 0.38 = 2.63
So $100 of gov spending → $263 of GDP.

⚠️ What reduces the multiplier (leakages): Saving (MPS = 1−MPC), Taxes (t), Imports (m). Each is money that "leaks" out of the spending cycle instead of recycling back into the domestic economy. Higher leakages = smaller multiplier.
⚠️ Paradox of Thrift

If every individual saves more (which seems wise), total spending in the economy falls → firms produce less → GDP falls → everyone's income falls. Individual prudence becomes collective harm. This is why government MUST spend during recessions even if households are saving.

AD/AS — The Big Picture Model

What is AD/AS?
AD/AS = Aggregate Demand / Aggregate Supply Model
This model shows the whole economy on one graph. The Y-axis is the price level (P) — like a "general inflation measure." The X-axis is real output (Y) — the GDP. Where AD meets AS = equilibrium: the price and output the economy settles at.
The Three Curves — What They Mean
  • AD (Aggregate Demand): Slopes DOWN. Higher prices → people are poorer in real terms → spend less. Lower prices → spend more. Simple.
  • AS (Aggregate Supply): Slopes UP. Higher prices → firms want to produce more (more profitable). Lower prices → produce less.
  • Y* (Long-run AS): Vertical line. In the long run, real output is fixed at potential — it doesn't depend on the price level. The economy always returns here eventually.

📊 AD/AS — Normal Equilibrium (start here)
AD slopes down (higher P = less demand). AS slopes up (higher P = more supply). They cross at E₀ — the economy's equilibrium price and output. Y* is where the economy should be in the long run.
📊 AD/AS — Stagflation (AS shifts LEFT)
AS₀ → AS₁ (left shift) = negative supply shock. New equilibrium E₁: HIGHER price P₁ AND LOWER output Y₁. This is stagflation — the worst of both worlds. Notice E₁ is up and left of E₀.
Fiscal Policy — Using G and T to Stabilise
  • Recession (Y < Y*): Raise G (build roads) or cut T (tax cuts) → AD shifts right → Y rises
  • Overheating (Y > Y*): Cut G or raise T → AD shifts left → Y falls

Why fiscal policy is slow: (1) Decision lag — parliament debates for months. (2) Execution lag — actually spending takes time. (3) Effect lag — multiplier takes quarters to work. By the time it kicks in, the recession may already be over.

Money, Banks & Money Creation

What is money?
Money = Anything widely accepted as payment
In modern economies, most "money" isn't physical cash — it's deposits in bank accounts created when banks make loans. The Central Bank (CB) controls the base, but commercial banks multiply it.
How banks create money — fractional reserve banking

You deposit $1000. The bank is required to keep only a fraction (say 20%) as reserves. It lends out $800. That $800 gets deposited in another bank, which keeps $160 and lends $640. And so on.

The total money created from your $1000 deposit:

Money Multiplier = 1 ÷ reserve ratio (v)
v = 20% → Multiplier = 1÷0.2 = 5 → $1000 creates $5000

Monetary Policy — How the Central Bank Controls the Economy

🏛️ The Central Bank's One Lever: The Overnight Interest Rate

The CB (Bank of Canada, ECB, Fed) sets the overnight rate — the rate banks charge each other for very short-term loans. This one rate sets the "floor" for all other rates in the economy. Within days, mortgage rates, business loan rates, and credit card rates all follow.

The Monetary Transmission Mechanism
How CB rate changes flow through the whole economy
This is the chain of events from a CB decision to the final effect on inflation. Memorise every step — this is exam Q2.
Step 1
CB raises overnight rate
Decision announced. Target rate goes up immediately.
↓
Step 2
ALL market rates rise
Mortgages, business loans, car loans, credit cards — all follow within days.
↓
Step 3
Investment (I) falls
Firms cancel/postpone capital projects — too expensive to finance at higher rates.
↓
Step 4
Consumption (C) falls
Households face higher mortgage payments. Borrowing to buy becomes more expensive.
↓
Step 5 (Open Economy)
Currency appreciates, NX falls
Higher rates attract foreign capital → demand for our currency rises → currency strengthens → our exports become expensive abroad → exports fall → NX↓.
↓
Step 6
AD shifts LEFT
↓I + ↓C + ↓NX = less total spending in the economy. Aggregate Demand curve shifts left.
↓
Step 7
Y falls below Y*, unemployment rises
Recessionary gap opens. Firms cut production, lay off workers.
↓
Step 8 — Result
Wages fall → costs fall → prices fall
Labour surplus drives wages down. Lower costs let firms lower prices. Inflation falls back toward 2% target.
🏛️ Monetary Transmission — CB Rate Hike Flow
This is the full chain from CB decision to inflation target. Each step takes time — which is why monetary policy effects are felt 12–18 months later.

Stagflation — The Impossible Dilemma

Definition
Stagflation
Simultaneous high inflation + falling output + rising unemployment. Caused by a negative supply shock (oil spike, supply chain crisis, war) that raises firms' costs — AS shifts LEFT. Price goes UP while output goes DOWN. The worst of both worlds.
⚡ Roubini's Dilemma — Why CBs Are Stuck

Normal recession: cut rates → demand recovers → problem solved.
Normal inflation: raise rates → demand falls → inflation cools → problem solved.
Stagflation: both at once. Whatever you do, you make one problem worse.

CB DecisionWhat happensVerdict
Raise ratesInflation cools ✓ BUT: demand falls further, recession deepens, debt servicing explodes, financial crash riskEconomic + financial crash
Don't raiseRecession softens ✓ BUT: inflation keeps rising, expectations de-anchor, wage-price spiral, permanently higher inflationPermanent inflation
Long-Run Money Neutrality

In the long run, printing more money only raises prices — it does NOT increase real output. Real output always returns to Y* regardless of how much money is printed. This is why CBs don't just print their way out of every problem.

What is a Peak? ▾
Highest point before GDP starts declining. Economy overheating — inflationary gap (Y>Y*). Unemployment very low, wages rising fast. Inflation building. CB may need to raise rates.
What is a Trough? ▾
Lowest point before recovery. Recessionary gap (Y<Y*). High unemployment, weak wages, idle factories. Government may use fiscal stimulus or CB may cut rates.
What is NAIRU? ▾
Natural Rate of Unemployment when Y=Y*. Includes frictional + structural only — zero cyclical unemployment. The lowest sustainable rate without causing inflation.
How to read the AD/AS graph

X = Real Output (Y). Y-axis = Price Level (P).

AD slopes down: higher prices → less demand. AS slopes up: higher prices → more supply. Y* is vertical: long-run potential output. Where AD meets AS = equilibrium.

AD / AS — Normal Equilibrium
E₀ = equilibrium where AD meets AS. Y* = long-run potential. Economy self-corrects toward Y* long-run.
What shifts AD? ▾
AD right (expansionary): ↑G, ↓T, ↑consumer confidence, ↓interest rates, ↑exports.

AD left (contractionary): CB raises rates, ↑T, ↓G, ↓confidence.
What shifts AS? ▾
AS right (good): ↓oil prices, ↓wages, better technology, ↑productivity.

AS left (bad — stagflation): ↑oil prices, supply chain crisis, ↑wages, trade tariffs raising input costs.
Why is long-run AS vertical? ▾
In the long run, real capacity (workers, factories, technology) doesn't change just because prices changed. Y always gravitates back to Y* through wage adjustment, regardless of the price level.
⚡ Stagflation — step by step
  1. Start at equilibrium E₀ (AD meets AS₀)
  2. Negative supply shock hits (oil spike / supply chain crisis)
  3. Firms' production costs rise → AS shifts LEFT to AS₁
  4. New equilibrium E₁: P rises AND Y falls
  5. Result = STAGFLATION — the worst of both worlds
Negative Supply Shock → Stagflation
AS₀→AS₁ (left shift) = negative supply shock. E₁ is UP-LEFT of E₀: higher P, lower Y. That's stagflation — and it's calculated precisely, not estimated.
Why can't normal policy fix stagflation? ▾
If CB raises rates to fight inflation → AD shifts left → Y falls even more → deeper recession. But inflation may fall.

If CB cuts rates to fight recession → AD shifts right → Y recovers → but inflation gets even worse.

Any demand-side policy fixes one problem but makes the other worse. This is Roubini's dilemma.
Real-world examples ▾
1970s Oil Crisis: OPEC quadrupled oil prices → stagflation across Western economies. Fed eventually raised rates to ~20% under Volcker (1980) — caused deep recession but broke inflation.

2021–2022 COVID + Ukraine: Supply chain disruptions + Russia-Ukraine war pushed oil/gas/food prices up → stagflation returned. ECB and Fed had to hike aggressively.
The Multiplier

The Keynesian Cross graph shows equilibrium where the AE line crosses the 45° line (Y=AE). A government spending increase shifts AE up → bigger increase in Y.

Keynesian Cross — Equilibrium & Multiplier Effect
Small ΔA (AE shifts up) → much bigger ΔY. Multiplier = ΔY ÷ ΔA = 1/(1−z). Each $1 of autonomous spending creates multiple $ of GDP.
Why is ΔY bigger than ΔA? ▾
Round 1: Gov spends $100. Round 2: That $100 becomes income, person spends $62 (z=0.62). Round 3: That $62 is income, next person spends $38.44. It keeps going. Total = $100 × 1/(1−0.62) = $263.
What reduces the multiplier? ▾
Leakages — money that exits the circular flow:
• Saving: MPS = 1 − MPC. Money saved doesn't circulate.
• Taxes: Reduces disposable income available to spend.
• Imports: Money spent on foreign goods leaks out of domestic economy.

Q1 — What is stagflation? What type of shock causes it?
Definition Q

Definition: Stagflation = stagnation + inflation simultaneously. It means: (1) rising or high inflation, (2) falling or stagnant output (negative/low GDP growth), and (3) rising unemployment — ALL at the same time.

Why unusual: In normal cycles, inflation and output move in the SAME direction. When demand rises, both output and prices rise. When demand falls, both fall. Stagflation is unusual because prices RISE while output FALLS — they move in OPPOSITE directions.

Cause — Negative Supply Shock: Something raises production costs for firms across the entire economy. Examples: oil price spike (OPEC 1973, Russia-Ukraine 2022), global supply chain disruptions (COVID-19), trade tariffs raising input costs.

Mechanism: Firms' costs rise → they must raise prices AND cut production → AS curve shifts LEFT → new equilibrium has HIGHER P and LOWER Y simultaneously.

AD/AS graph: AS₀ → AS₁ (leftward shift). New equilibrium E₁ is up-left of E₀: higher price P₁, lower output Y₁. AD curve is unchanged.

Q2 — How does the CB reduce inflation to 2%? Explain the transmission mechanism.
Mechanism Q

Policy: Contractionary monetary policy — CB raises the overnight interest rate.

Full mechanism (8 steps):

  1. CB raises overnight target rate
  2. All market rates follow: mortgages, business loans, credit cards
  3. Investment falls: firms cancel/postpone capital projects (too costly)
  4. Consumption falls: households face higher mortgage payments, less credit
  5. Open economy: higher rates attract foreign capital → currency appreciates → exports become expensive abroad → net exports (NX) fall
  6. AD shifts LEFT: I↓ + C↓ + NX↓ = less aggregate spending
  7. Y falls below Y*: recessionary gap opens, unemployment rises
  8. Labour market slack → wages fall → firm costs fall → prices fall → inflation returns to 2% ✓

Key caveat: There are long lags. Effects are felt 12–18 months after the decision. This makes timing very difficult.

Q3 — What does "behind the curve" mean? Why is it dangerous?
Concept Q

Definition: "Behind the curve" = the CB was too slow to raise rates. By the time it acts, inflation is already well above target and entrenched in expectations.

Why it's dangerous — 3 mechanisms:

  1. De-anchoring of expectations: If people stop believing the CB will deliver 2% inflation, they build higher expected inflation into wages and contracts. This becomes self-fulfilling — expected inflation drives actual inflation.
  2. Wage-price spiral: Workers see prices rising → demand higher wages → firms face higher costs → raise prices further → workers demand even higher wages. Once started, very hard to stop without causing a deep recession.
  3. Loss of credibility: CB credibility is what allows it to influence expectations cheaply. Once lost, restoring it requires far more painful rate hikes. Historical proof: Fed under Volcker (1980) had to raise rates to 20% to break the 1970s inflation — causing the deepest US recession since the Great Depression.

Implication: Early, pre-emptive action is always less costly than delayed, reactive action. The longer you wait, the bigger the hike needed, and the bigger the recession caused.

Q4 — Why do sharp rate hikes cause both economic AND financial crash?
Analysis Q

Economic crash (via transmission mechanism):

Sharp rate hikes → severe fall in I, C, NX → AD collapses → deep recession → unemployment soars. This is the standard economic channel.

Financial crash — the Debt Trap:

Years of ultra-low interest rates (2009–2021) encouraged households, firms, and governments to take on massive debt. When rates were low, debt was easy to service. Sharp rate hikes change everything:

  1. Debt servicing costs explode (variable rate debt reprices immediately)
  2. Over-leveraged firms cannot pay → defaults begin
  3. Bank losses accumulate → banks restrict lending (credit crunch)
  4. Less credit → even less spending → recession deepens
  5. More defaults → more bank losses → deeper credit crunch → vicious cycle

Roubini's argument: The combination of high private AND public debt means sharp hikes that would have been manageable in the 1980s are now capable of triggering a financial crisis on top of the economic recession — a "double whammy."

Q5 — Why does NOT raising rates lead to permanently higher inflation?
Analysis Q

Three compounding mechanisms:

  1. De-anchoring of inflation expectations: The CB's 2% target works only because people believe it. If the CB doesn't act → inflation stays high → people update their expectations upward → they build 5–8% inflation into wage negotiations and long-term contracts → actual inflation stays permanently elevated. A credible threat to act is itself disinflationary.
  2. Wage-price spiral: Rising prices → workers demand higher nominal wages to protect real purchasing power → higher wages = higher firm costs → firms raise prices → workers demand more. Without intervention, this spiral is self-sustaining and accelerates over time.
  3. Long-run money neutrality: Loose monetary policy (not hiking) is effectively printing money. In the long run, this only raises the price level — it does NOT increase real output. Real GDP returns to Y* regardless. So the CB just permanently buys a higher price level with no long-run output gain.

Conclusion: Not hiking avoids short-run recession pain but accepts permanently higher structural inflation, which is harder to eradicate and requires even more painful treatment later. There is no free lunch.

Q6 — MCQ: What is the multiplier and how do you calculate it?
Calculation Q

Formula: Multiplier = 1 ÷ (1 − z), where z = MPC×(1−t) − m

Example: MPC=0.8, t=0.1, m=0.1
z = 0.8×0.9 − 0.1 = 0.72 − 0.1 = 0.62
Multiplier = 1 ÷ 0.38 = 2.63

Meaning: Every $1 of government spending increases GDP by $2.63.

What reduces it: Higher taxes (t↑), higher savings (MPC↓), higher imports (m↑) — all leakages from the circular flow.

Q7 — MCQ: What is the money multiplier and how does fractional reserve banking work?
Calculation Q

Formula: Money Multiplier = 1 ÷ reserve ratio (v)

Example: v = 20%: Multiplier = 1÷0.2 = 5. A $1000 deposit creates $5000 of total money in the banking system.

How it works: Bank keeps $200 (20%), lends $800 → deposited elsewhere → keeps $160, lends $640 → … Total = $1000 × 5 = $5000.

Key insight: Most money in modern economies is not printed by the CB — it's created by commercial banks when they make loans.

Q8 — MCQ: How do fiscal policy lags make it hard to stabilise the economy?
Concept Q

Fiscal policy (changing G and T) works in theory, but three lags make precise timing nearly impossible:

  1. Decision lag: Parliament/government must debate and vote on spending changes. This can take months — especially in a political crisis.
  2. Execution lag: Once approved, the spending takes time to actually happen. A new road or hospital doesn't get built overnight.
  3. Effect lag: Even once money is flowing, the multiplier effect ripples through the economy over many quarters.

The problem: By the time fiscal stimulus is fully felt, the recession may already be over — and now the stimulus is causing an inflationary gap instead. This is why fiscal policy is better for large, long downturns (like the 2008 crisis) than for fine-tuning short-run fluctuations.

Monetary policy is faster — a CB rate decision takes effect in financial markets within hours and the real economy within 6–12 months — which is why it's the primary short-run stabilisation tool.
